World War II, which lasted from 1939 to 1945, was a global conflict that involved most of the world's nations, including all of the great powers. It was the most widespread war in history and directly involved more than 100 million people from over 30 countries. The major participants threw their entire economic, industrial, and scientific capabilities behind the war effort, erasing the distinction between civilian and military resources. The war began on September 1, 1939, when Germany, led by Adolf Hitler, invaded Poland. This act prompted Britain and France to declare war on Germany. The conflict had several root causes, including the Treaty of Versailles' harsh terms imposed on Germany after World War I, economic instability during the Great Depression, and the rise of totalitarian regimes in Germany, Italy, and Japan.

The war saw significant fighting in multiple theaters. In Europe, Germany quickly conquered much of Western Europe using a tactic known as Blitzkrieg or "lightning war," which involved rapid, surprise attacks. The largest and dead liest theater of the war was the Eastern Front, where Nazi Germany and the Soviet Union engaged in brutal combat after Germany's invasion of the Soviet Union in June 1941. In the Pacific, the conflict was dominated by the struggle between Japan and the Allies, primarily the United States. This theater saw significant naval battles, island-hopping campaigns, and harsh combat conditions.

Key events of the war included Germany's swift conquests in 1940, including the fall of France and the Battle of Britain, where the UK successfully resisted German air assaults. The war expanded dramatically in 1941 with Operation Barbarossa, the German invasion of the Soviet Union, and the Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor, which brought the United States into the conflict. The Allies began to turn the tide in 1942-1943 with pivotal victories such as the Battle of Midway in the Pacific and the Battle of Stalingrad on the Eastern Front. In 1944, D-Day marked a significant turning point as Allied forces landed in Normandy, France, beginning the liberation of Western Europe. The war in Europe ended in May 1945 with Germany's surrender following Hitler's suicide. In the Pacific, the war concluded after the United States dropped atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki in August 1945, leading to Japan's surrender on September 2, 1945.

The consequences of World War II were profound and far-reaching. Over 70 million people died, including six million Jews who were murdered in the Holocaust. The war led to significant geopolitical changes, including the establishment of the United Nations to foster international cooperation and prevent future conflicts. It also set the stage for the Cold War between the United States and the Soviet Union. Economically, many European and Asian countries were left in ruins, necessitating extensive rebuilding efforts such as the Marshall Plan in Europe. World War II reshaped the global order, leading to significant changes in political boundaries, economic systems, and international relations.
